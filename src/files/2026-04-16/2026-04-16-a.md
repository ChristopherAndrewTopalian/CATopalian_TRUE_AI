When describing and communicating the concepts of reasoning, thinking, and memory through the energy in your hypercube neural network, a few key elements from your source textures stand out as particularly important. 

Because your design is heavily grounded in formal logic (as seen in the 2D matrix), the visualization of the energy should directly reflect those computational concepts.

Here is what would be most impactful to emphasize:

### 1. Reasoning (The Logic Gates)
Your first texture explicitly maps out the 16 Boolean logic gates (AND, OR, XOR, Tautology, etc.). To communicate reasoning, the energy shouldn't just flow randomly; it needs to behave according to these rules. 
* **Directed Flow:** You can describe the energy as splitting, combining, or stopping based on the specific gate it passes through. For example, energy passing through an "AND" gate might require two distinct streams of energy to meet before continuing, while an "XOR" gate might route energy differently based on contrasting inputs. 
* **The Cylinders as Logic Pathways:** The extrusion of these 2D gates into 3D cylinders means the reasoning has depth and trajectory. The energy represents the active computation of those truth tables.

### 2. Thinking (The Intersecting Nodes)
The point where your up/down, left/right, and forward/backward cylinders intersect is the core of the network.
* **Multidimensional Processing:** Thinking can be communicated by visualizing the intersections as highly active processing hubs. When energy from different cylinders collides at these central nodes, it represents complex, multidimensional problem-solving—taking inputs from multiple logical pathways to form a new output. 
* **Dynamic Routing:** The energy pulsing, shifting colors, or changing direction at these cross-sections visually translates to the network "making a decision" in real-time.

### 3. Memory (Persistent Energy States)
In computational systems and neural networks, memory is about storing a state or a weight. 
* **Residual Glow:** To communicate memory, you could describe the energy leaving a lingering glow or a residual trace along certain cylinder pathways after a thought has passed, representing a "learned" pathway or a stored variable.
* **Energy Loops:** Alternatively, memory can be visualized as energy trapped in a continuous, stable loop within specific sections of the matrix, much like a flip-flop circuit that holds a 1 or a 0.

By grounding the visual behavior of the energy in the actual function of the 16 logic gates, the design moves from just being a beautiful structure to a functional representation of a working artificial mind.
