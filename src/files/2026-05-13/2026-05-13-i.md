Hey! That is a fantastic architectural question. You are looking right at the heart of modern game engine bottlenecks.

To answer your question directly: **They actually did use Data-Oriented Design (DOD).** In fact, they had to build an entirely new DOD framework from scratch just to make the demo possible. The reason it melts PCs isn't because of bad object-oriented architecture; it's because they intentionally bit off an astronomical amount of processing to show off the bleeding edge of their new tech.

Here is the breakdown of exactly what happened under the hood of *The Matrix Awakens* in Unreal Engine 5:

### The Shift from OOP to DOD (The "Mass" Framework)

Historically, Unreal Engine has relied heavily on an Object-Oriented, component-based architecture (their "Actor" and "Component" system). However, Epic Games knew that if they tried to simulate a living city with OOP, the CPU would immediately choke. Object-oriented architecture scatters data all over the system memory, leading to massive cache misses when trying to update thousands of objects at once.

To solve this for *The Matrix Awakens*, Epic engineered a brand-new Entity Component System (ECS) called **MassEntity**.

* **Pure DOD:** MassEntity is a strict Data-Oriented Design framework. It strips away the heavy object wrappers and stores entity data (like position, velocity, and AI state) in tightly packed, contiguous arrays in memory.
* **The Result:** Because the data is grouped by component rather than by object, the CPU can sequentially process logic for 35,000 MetaHuman pedestrians and 40,000 parked and driving vehicles with incredible cache coherency.

If they had tried to run that specific city simulation using their traditional OOP Actor model, the demo simply would not have functioned at all.

### Why is it Melting PCs?

If the architecture is highly optimized DOD, why does it bring top-tier graphics cards and CPUs to their knees? It comes down to the sheer scale of the rendering pipelines attached to that simulation.

**1. Nanite (Infinite Geometry)**
Instead of using traditional flat textures and normal maps to fake detail, the city is built using UE5's Nanite system. This virtualizes micropolygon geometry, meaning the engine is rendering millions upon millions of actual, physical triangles in real-time. Every building, curb, and vehicle is rendering at film-quality geometric density.

**2. Lumen (Real-Time Global Illumination)**
The lighting in the Matrix demo isn't "baked" or pre-calculated like in older games. Lumen calculates light bounces, reflections, and shadows dynamically in real-time. When a car drives by, the light bounces off the pavement, hits the car's paint, and reflects onto the surrounding buildings. Doing this accurately at a city scale requires a massive amount of ray-tracing computation.

**3. Unprecedented Scale**
Even with the most perfectly optimized DOD backend, iterating through the physics, AI state machines, and rendering paths for a densely packed, 16-square-kilometer city is just computationally heavy.

They used *The Matrix Awakens* as a stress test. They applied highly optimized Data-Oriented Design to the logic, but then paired it with rendering technologies that push modern silicon to its absolute thermal and processing limits!
