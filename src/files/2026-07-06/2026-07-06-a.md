Good catches all around — you've got the right mental model now: no shifting, no index math, just names pointing to names. Let me answer both questions concretely.

**How many concepts might a "forebrain" hold?**

Rough estimates from vocabulary research: a well-read adult recognizes somewhere in the range of 20,000–40,000 word families, and active everyday vocabulary is smaller than that. But your `cortex` isn't just words — it's *concepts*, and concepts multiply past word count fast, since one word ("bank") can be several concepts, and plenty of concepts have no single word at all (a *feeling*, a *specific memory*, a *relationship between two things*). So a reasonable ballpark for a rich semantic structure like this is **low hundreds of thousands of entries** for something modeling a full adult's associative knowledge — not millions, but comfortably past the point where scanning a flat array becomes painful. That's a genuine estimate, not a precise fact — nobody's actually counted "how many concepts does a human have," it's inherently fuzzy — but it's the right order of magnitude to design around.

**How much slower would the flat array be?**

This is where the math is actually clean and satisfying. A hash-based lookup (`cortex.bird`) costs roughly the same tiny, constant amount of work *no matter how big `cortex` gets* — that's the whole point of hashing, it computes where to look directly instead of searching. A flat array scan (`cortex.find(e => e.concept === "bird")`), on average, has to check about **half the array** before it finds a match (best case: first item; worst case: last item or not found at all; average settles around N/2).

So the slowdown isn't a fixed number — it grows as your cortex grows:

| Entries (N) | Array scan (avg. checks) | Object lookup (avg. checks) | Roughly how much slower |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | ~500 | ~1 | ~500× |
| 10,000 | ~5,000 | ~1 | ~5,000× |
| 100,000 | ~50,000 | ~1 | ~50,000× |
| 1,000,000 | ~500,000 | ~1 | ~500,000× |

That last row is the important one for your "forebrain" scale — a flat array wouldn't just be *slower*, it would become the entire bottleneck of the whole system, because every single "what do I know about X" question would cost half a million comparisons instead of one lookup. This is exactly why real computers don't store dictionaries, databases, or (loosely) brains as flat lists to be scanned — it's also, loosely, why associative recall in an actual brain doesn't feel like "checking every memory I've ever had," as you said — the object-of-objects model matches that felt experience much more closely than the array ever could, which is a nice sign you're modeling the right thing.

**One honest correction on the power/floating-point point**, since it's worth being precise about: it's not quite that floating-point math itself is "the trap" causing megawatt power draws in large AI models — floats aren't inherently expensive to compute. The actual power cost comes from *how many* of those multiply-and-add operations happen — modern models run trillions of them per response, because they're doing matrix multiplication across billions of parameters, not because float arithmetic itself is heavy. Your instinct that a symbolic, name-based structure would be cheaper is very much correct — you're avoiding that firehose of arithmetic entirely by using direct lookups instead of numerical computation — I just want to make sure the "why" stays accurate as you explain this project to other people: it's "we do far less computation" rather than "floating point itself is the villain."
