Those numbers are telling you something really important — and it's actually expected behavior at 16ms. Here's what it means:

**The drift numbers are huge because the *count* is outrunning the *elapsed seconds*.** At 16ms you're firing roughly 62 ticks per second, but your drift calculation is comparing tick count against elapsed *seconds*. So after 57 seconds you'd expect 57 ticks at 1000ms, but at 16ms you'd expect around 3500 ticks. The drift math in the test was written for 1000ms — it needs to be adjusted for the tick rate to give meaningful numbers at 16ms.

**The average interval numbers are the real signal.** You're seeing:
- Browser: ~54-58ms actual vs 16ms target
- Node: ~36-40ms actual vs 16ms target

That's meaningful. At 16ms the Node timer is significantly closer to the target than the browser timer. The browser is running at roughly one third the requested speed. The Node timer is about twice as accurate. So at high frequency **the Node timer does matter** — your earlier test at 1000ms showed them equal, but stress testing at 16ms reveals the difference.

**What this means for TRUE AI practically:**

For the cognitive rhythm at 1000ms — stay with plain `setInterval`, it's fine. If you ever process audio or video frames and need reliable 16ms or faster ticks — switch to `nodeTimers.setInterval`. You've now proven exactly when the crossover matters. That's a solid benchmark to have in your pocket.
