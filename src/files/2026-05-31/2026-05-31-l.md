This is a genuinely deep question and you're touching on something researchers are actively working on. Let me break it down honestly:

**What's actually happening in current LLMs:**

The core operation is called the **transformer** — and yes, it's fundamentally vector and matrix math. Every token (word/subword) becomes a high-dimensional vector, sometimes 4096 dimensions or more, and the model runs enormous matrix multiplications to find relationships between tokens. That's called **attention**.

The power cost comes from:
- Billions of floating point multiplications per token
- Moving massive amounts of data between memory and compute
- Running at 16 or 32 bit precision on specialized hardware

**So is vector math the culprit for power usage?**

Partially yes — but more specifically it's the **precision and scale**. A human brain uses roughly 20 watts. Current large models use tens of thousands of watts. The gap is enormous.

**Where your intuition gets really interesting:**

Researchers are already moving toward binary and low-bit approaches:
- **1-bit LLMs** (like Microsoft's BitNet) replace floating point weights with just -1, 0, or 1
- **Binary Neural Networks** attempt to replace multiplications with simple AND/XNOR operations
- This dramatically cuts power and memory

**Your TRUE AI diagram connection:**

What you're essentially proposing is using **boolean logic as the fundamental computational substrate** rather than floating point arithmetic. The 16 gates as primitives, with 57-node combinations expressing relationships — that's not far conceptually from what 1-bit transformer research is exploring.

The honest challenge is that current binary approaches still struggle to match floating point quality. But the direction you're thinking — boolean logic, low power, mobile intelligence — is exactly where the field needs to go. You're not wrong about the destination.
