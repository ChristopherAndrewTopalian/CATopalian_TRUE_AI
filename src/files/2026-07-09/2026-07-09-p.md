There's a genuinely sharp idea buried in here, and also a mixup worth untangling — let's find the real gem first, because it's a good one.

**The part that's actually correct and clever: XOR/XNOR between two bit-vectors is a real, well-established similarity technique**, and it's much cheaper than what you're doing now. If `dog` and `cat` are each a string of bits, XOR-ing them position by position and counting the differences gives you what's called **Hamming distance** — a real, standard measure of similarity between binary vectors, used in things like fingerprinting and fast approximate search (SimHash is a real-world example). XNOR gives you the complement — count of *agreements* instead of differences. Critically: **this is bitwise, integer-only, and dramatically cheaper than the floating-point division your current `resonance` calculation does.** So if what you mean is "compare two words by XOR-ing their bit-vectors and counting matches," that's not just plausible, it's a genuinely good, real technique, and it would actually replace your `matchCount / smallestLength` float math with pure integer operations. That part of your instinct is sound and worth building.

**Here's the mixup:** the 16 logic gates (tautology, contradiction, AND, OR, XOR, XNOR, NAND, NOR, etc.) are *operations between two inputs* — they don't make sense as a fixed label sitting at position 3 of a single word's own vector. XNOR isn't a property `dog` has; XNOR is something you *do* between `dog`'s bits and `cat`'s bits to find out how alike they are. So "position 3 is XNOR, and that's the similarity detector" only makes sense as "apply XNOR between two words' vectors," not as "dog has a 1 or 0 sitting in its own personal XNOR slot." That distinction matters a lot for how you'd actually code this — I'd fix that before building further, since it changes what the 56 bits are even for.

**On "training begins and it flips the bits" — I want to flag something important:** the moment you say the bits get set through training rather than you hand-assigning them, you've walked back into needing a neural network, full stop. Training bits to encode learned relationships *is* what a (binarized) neural network does — there's a real field called Binarized Neural Networks that does exactly this, using 1-bit weights instead of floats specifically to cut power costs, and it's a legitimate, actively researched efficiency technique. So if that's the direction you want, you're not replacing neural networks, you're describing a specific, cheaper *kind* of one. If instead you hand-assign all 56 bits yourself for every word, you're back to manual authoring — cheaper to run, yes, but you've reintroduced the exact limitation from before: it only knows what you typed, and it still won't handle a word it's never seen.

**One more thing worth reconciling before you build:** you said 56 bits (which correctly gives 2^56 ≈ 72 quadrillion — your arithmetic there is right), but also 16 gates + 40 intersection nodes + 1 center neuron = 57. Those two numbers don't match, and it matters which one is real before you lay out the array — worth deciding definitively rather than letting both floating around.

**So, direct answer:** no, this hasn't defeated the floating-point/megawatt problem in the way you're hoping — but it *has* landed on something real: bitwise integer comparison (XOR/XNOR/Hamming) is a legitimate, much cheaper alternative to your current float division, and that part is worth keeping and building for real. The megawatt cost was never really about "floats vs. integers" as a storage choice — it's about the sheer scale of data and training needed to make a system generalize to things nobody explicitly told it. Binary representations can cut *inference* cost significantly (that's real, it's why the industry researches them) — they don't remove the cost of *learning* meaningful representations in the first place, if that's still something you want the system to do.
