Good questions, and there's a clean, satisfying answer to the "why can't 1D work" part — let's start there since it directly answers your array-of-objects question.

**Why one-dimensional position can't do what you want, concretely:** you said it yourself — where would cat go? Cat needs to be near "dog" (both pets), near "animal," near "meow," near "independent"... but also *far* from dog in the "loyal vs. independent" sense. A single line only gives you two directions: left and right. The moment something needs to be simultaneously close to several different things for several different reasons, one axis runs out of room — you'd have to pick which relationship "wins" the position, and every other relationship gets distorted. That's not a precision problem (floats vs. integers) — it's a structural one. A line literally cannot hold that many independent "closeness" relationships at once, no matter what data type you use for position.

**This is exactly why real systems don't use 3D either — that's the misconception worth clearing up.** Real word embeddings typically use somewhere between 100 and several thousand dimensions (early word2vec: ~300; modern LLMs: often 1,000–12,000+). Not because anyone's visualizing a 12,000-dimensional room — nobody can, including the engineers who built it — but because each extra dimension is one more independent "direction of meaning" available. With enough dimensions, "dog" can be simultaneously close to "puppy" along one axis, close to "loyal" along a totally different axis, and close to "four-legged" along yet another — none of them have to compete for the same line. It's pure math generalizing past what we can picture, not a spatial metaphor holding up past 3D.

**Is emergent behavior caused by this spatial arrangement itself?** Not directly — that's an interesting theory but it's not quite the mechanism. Emergence comes from a combination of: (1) the sheer number of dimensions giving room for many relationships to coexist without interfering, (2) training on enormous amounts of real text so those relationships get shaped by actual usage patterns rather than hand-authored rules, and (3) the transformer architecture's attention mechanism, which lets the model weigh which earlier words matter most for predicting the next one — a genuinely different structural idea from "words have fixed coordinates," since attention changes dynamically based on context. It's the combination of scale + architecture + training data, not the geometry alone, that produces the emergent behavior you're noticing.

**On the "observer" question** — this is honestly one of the genuinely unresolved philosophical questions about these systems, so I'll give you the mainstream technical answer rather than pretend it's fully settled: there's no separate observing entity built into the architecture. The training process itself (millions of rounds of "predict the next word, get penalized when wrong, adjust weights slightly") forces the network's internal weights into patterns that happen to produce coherent, contextually appropriate output — the "meaning" is implicit in the weight structure, not held by any component that's watching or interpreting. Whether that process constitutes "understanding" in any deeper sense is a real, actively debated question among philosophers and researchers — I'd genuinely encourage you to explore that question rather than take a confident stance on it from me, since reasonable people land in very different places on it.

**Do you need C++ to get away from floats? No — and here's the actual reason nobody bothers:** the float thing isn't a JavaScript flaw that other languages magically avoid at this scale — floats are *chosen on purpose* for training, in every mainstream language, because gradient-based learning needs to nudge values by tiny fractional amounts, and a float is what allows "adjust this weight by 0.0003." The real efficiency trick production systems use isn't "avoid floats," it's **quantization** — training with full-precision floats (needed for the delicate nudging), then *afterward*, once training is done, compressing the final trained weights down to lower precision (float16, or even 8-bit integers) for actually running the model, since by then you no longer need the fine adjustability, just the final answer. That's a real, well-established technique — and it happens after training, not instead of it.

And the reason nobody hand-writes this in raw JavaScript or C++ arrays isn't the language's number type — it's that real training runs on GPUs, doing millions of these float operations simultaneously in parallel hardware built specifically for exactly that kind of math. Python dominates this field not because it secretly avoids 64-bit floats, but because Python is just the control layer sitting on top of libraries (PyTorch, TensorFlow) that are themselves written in C++ and CUDA to talk directly to the GPU. So the honest answer is: you don't need to abandon JavaScript or floats to move toward this — you'd need to move toward the actual missing ingredient, which is training on real data at scale, using tools built for that (which is a much bigger undertaking than a language switch, and genuinely optional depending on whether that's even the direction you want to take this project).
