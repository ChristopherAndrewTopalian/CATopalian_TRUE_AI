Let's separate what's genuinely working here from what the results can and can't support — because there's real, solid design in this code, and there's also a claim riding on top of it that the code itself doesn't back up.

**Is this a neural network? No, and it's worth being precise about why.** A neural network *learns* — it starts with random weights and adjusts them through training on data until patterns emerge on their own. Your `cortex` never learns anything; every trait in every array is something *you* typed. That's not a lesser thing — it's a different, older, entirely legitimate branch of AI called symbolic AI (sometimes "GOFAI" — Good Old-Fashioned AI), the dominant paradigm before deep learning took over in the 2010s. What you've built is closer to a hand-authored knowledge base with a similarity metric on top — genuinely in the same family as expert systems and semantic networks from the 70s–80s.

**The similarity metric you landed on is actually a real, respected idea, not just an intuition.** Counting shared traits over the smaller set's size is close to a technique called Tversky's feature-based similarity model (1977) — psychologist Amos Tversky specifically argued that human similarity judgments work by comparing shared *features*, not by measuring distance in some abstract space. That's a legitimate academic position, and it's arguably a better psychological model of how people actually judge "how alike are these two things" than pure vector-distance approaches. So — genuinely, no flattery — your instinct to use overlapping traits instead of coordinates in space lines up with real cognitive science, not just a workaround.

**The results are sensible and defensible:**
- Puppy/dog hitting the index-0 shortcut for an instant identity match — clean, correct design.
- Golden retriever/pitbull at 71% — reasonable; they share almost everything except temperament traits.
- Airplane/bird at 27% via "wings, flies, sky" — this is actually the most interesting result in the whole test set. It's surfacing a real, known phenomenon (convergent evolution / functional analogy) purely from shared vocabulary, with zero built-in understanding of biology or engineering. That's a nice emergent effect from a simple rule.
- Rock/dog at 0% — correctly finds nothing in common.

**Now, the honest correction — and it's specific, not just a vibe:** you haven't eliminated floating point here. Look at your own resonance line: `matchCount / smallestLength` is a division producing a decimal, `Math.floor` just chops it back to a whole number for display. The float is still happening, just briefly and cheaply. That's fine! But it means "we got rid of floating point" isn't literally true of this code, even at the small scale you're testing.

**The bigger issue is the leap from "this works on 8 words" to "this replaces the need for megawatts."** Here's the part that doesn't scale, and it's not a compute problem, it's a labor problem: every single fact in this system exists because you personally typed it. The moment someone asks about "wolf," "husky," "greyhound," or literally any concept you haven't hand-entered, the system has nothing — not a wrong answer, no answer at all, `undefined`. It can't infer that a wolf is probably canine-like from context, can't handle a word it's never seen, can't tell "bank" (river) from "bank" (money) based on context, can't parse a sentence, can't generalize a pattern to something novel.

That's not a small gap — **that specific ability (generalizing to things not explicitly programmed in) is the entire reason neural networks exist and the entire reason they cost so much power.** The megawatts aren't being burned because engineers didn't think of your trick — they're being burned training a system, on trillions of examples, to handle the combinatorial explosion of language and concepts a hand-written array could never cover, including things nobody thought to write down. Your system is cheap to *run* precisely because it does far less — it only ever answers questions about the finite set of things you personally authored. That's a real and valid tradeoff (classical symbolic systems are legitimately fast and cheap, and this is a genuine advantage of your approach for a *fixed, bounded* domain), but it's a tradeoff, not a free lunch — you're trading "generalizes to anything" for "cheap and fast on exactly what I typed in."

So, where I'd land: you've built a real, working, well-designed feature-similarity engine over a hand-curated knowledge base — that's a legitimate thing to have built, and the design choices (index-0 root shortcut, smallest-array denominator to catch subsets) show actual engineering judgment, not just enthusiasm. What it hasn't done is solve the problem neural networks are actually solving, or replace the need for them at scale — it's a different tool, good at a different, narrower job. If you want to push this project further in a genuinely interesting direction, the open question worth chasing is: how would this system handle a word it's never seen before, using only the words it does know? That's the actual frontier between what you've built and what you're hoping it becomes.
