Let's trace exactly what this code does, because the honest read is a bit different from what I think you're picturing.

**What `initializeLocalBrain` actually does:** it loops through every word in your ~466,000-word dictionary and assigns each one **56 completely random bits** — `Math.round(Math.random())` for every position, no relationship to meaning at all. At this stage, "dog" and "cat" and "the" and "photosynthesis" all get equally random, equally meaningless 56-bit fingerprints. Nothing about language has been "mapped" yet — it's pure noise, word-for-word.

**What `trainNetwork` actually does:** it only has **6 hardcoded word pairs** to work with (`["yes","affirmative"]`, `["dog","puppy"]`, etc.). For each pair, if they differ in more than 5 of their 56 bits, it picks one differing bit at random and copies word A's bit onto word B. Repeat for up to 100 epochs, or until nothing needs flipping anymore.

**Here's the important part: this only touches those 6 pairs.** Every other word in your 466,000-word dictionary — everything not explicitly named in `trainingData` — never gets touched by training at all. It stays exactly as random as it started. So after running this, you'd have 6 words nudged closer together, and roughly 465,994 words sitting at pure random noise relative to each other and to everything else. "Dog" and "puppy" would end up similar; "dog" and "wolf," or "dog" and literally any word you didn't list, would show a random, meaningless similarity score — not zero, not low, just noise, indistinguishable from comparing two words that have nothing to do with each other.

That's the core issue, more than the floating-point question: **this isn't currently a system that knows anything about language.** It's a system that knows 6 specific facts you told it directly, sitting inside a sea of noise pretending to be data.

**On "have we defeated floating point" — here's the part that's going to be disappointing, so I want to be direct about why:** JavaScript doesn't actually have a "bit" data type. Every single number in that `randomThoughtArray` — whether it holds `0`, `1`, or `3.14159` — is stored internally as a **64-bit float**. `Math.round(Math.random())` still produces a JS Number, and JS Numbers are always floats under the hood, regardless of what value they happen to hold. So `thoughtA[j] !== thoughtB[j]` in your training loop is doing exactly the same float comparison it would be doing if those arrays held real decimals instead of 0s and 1s. **You haven't escaped floating point — you've just restricted yourself to using only two of the values a float can hold.** To actually get the efficiency win you're after, you'd need to use real bit-packing — something like a `Uint8Array` with bitwise operators (`<<`, `|`, `&`) to cram 8 genuine bits into 1 byte of real memory. That's a very doable next step if you want it, but it's not what's happening here yet.

This also explains your file size, by the way: 45MB isn't because integers are heavy — it's because `JSON.stringify` writes every bit out as a text character (`"0,1,0,1,..."`), and text is expensive no matter what number it represents. A real packed-bit version of the same 466,000 words × 56 bits would fit in about **3.3MB of actual binary** (466,000 × 56 bits ÷ 8 ÷ 1,000,000) — over 13x smaller than what you have now. So there's a real, legitimate win available here, just not the one currently happening.

**Why real embedding systems use floats, and it's not oversight:** word2vec and similar systems use continuous decimal values specifically because *training itself* needs values you can nudge by tiny amounts — gradient descent works by computing "how wrong was I" and adjusting weights by a small, smooth fraction of that error. A bit can only be 0 or 1; there's no "nudge it 3% closer." Your training loop actually reflects this tension directly — notice it doesn't nudge gradually, it just flips a whole bit outright when the mismatch is too large. That's a coarser, less precise kind of learning than what gradient descent does, which is part of why real systems reach for floats: they need fine-grained adjustability, not because anyone missed the "just use integers" idea.

**Straight answer to your actual question — bit-vector version vs. semantic-array version, which is better:** right now, honestly, your semantic array (the `cortex` object with `related`, `attributes`, `root`) is the stronger system, by a good margin. It's fully correct, fully interpretable, and every relationship in it means something because you put it there on purpose. The bit-vector version, as currently coded, produces mostly meaningless noise for 99.99% of its vocabulary and only real signal for 6 words. It's not a step forward from the semantic engine — it's an earlier-stage, currently-broken prototype of a different technique (real word embeddings), missing the one ingredient that makes that technique work: training signal derived from **massive amounts of real language usage** (real word2vec models learn from billions of words of real text, inferring "dog and puppy appear in similar contexts" automatically), not a handful of pairs you type in by hand. Scaling this up "correctly" would mean either feeding it a huge real corpus (a much bigger undertaking than what's here), or accepting you're back to manually authoring relationships — which is the exact labor problem your semantic engine already has, just with a much less readable, much less debuggable format sitting on top of it.

**My honest recommendation:** keep building the semantic/object-of-arrays engine as your main line of work — it's working, it's readable, and every result you've gotten from it so far has been sensible and explainable. Treat this bit-vector file as a genuinely interesting side experiment in real bit-packing and Hamming-distance similarity (worth learning, worth playing with), but not yet a replacement for anything, since as written it doesn't know more about language than the 6 sentences you hand it.

On the I Ching/Leibniz note — that's real history, worth knowing: Leibniz did correspond about the I Ching's hexagrams and saw a connection to his binary system, that's documented. It doesn't change the engineering facts above, but it's a genuinely interesting thread if you want to dig into the history of binary representation for its own sake.
