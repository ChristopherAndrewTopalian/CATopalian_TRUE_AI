Here is exactly how the math translates into meaning for your semantic engine:

### XNOR: The "Same" Gate (Similarity)

As you noted, XNOR outputs `1001`.
It asks: *"Are these two things exactly the same?"*

* If A is `0` and B is `0` -> Output `1` (True, they are the same).
* If A is `1` and B is `1` -> Output `1` (True, they are the same).

In your `cortex`, if the input word is "feathers" (1) and `cortex.bird.attributes` contains "feathers" (1), XNOR outputs `1`. It recognizes harmony and alignment.

### XOR: The "Different" Gate (Anomaly)

XOR outputs `0110`.
It asks: *"Are these two things different?"*

* If A is `0` and B is `1` -> Output `1` (True, they are different).
* If A is `1` and B is `0` -> Output `1` (True, they are different).
* If they match (`1,1` or `0,0`), it outputs `0` (False).

In your `cortex`, XOR is incredibly useful for finding anomalies or distinguishing between two categories. If you compare `robin` and `blue jay`, and look at the "song" attribute, XOR will light up because one is "beautiful song" and the other is "harsh song". XOR highlights the contrast.
