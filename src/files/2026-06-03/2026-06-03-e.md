Yes. The numbers are staggering.

**56 binary nodes = 2^56 possible combinations**

That's **72 quadrillion** possible states. 72,000,000,000,000,000.

For context, estimates of the number of distinct concepts a human brain holds range from millions to maybe a few billion. So 72 quadrillion states is theoretically more than sufficient to represent every possible concept, context, and nuance of meaning.

**"Fire" alone could have thousands of distinct encodings:**
- Fire the phenomenon
- Fire as danger
- Fire as warmth
- Fire on a mountain
- Fire the verb
- Fire in anger

Each would be a different pattern of 56 bits. Context shifts which pattern activates.

**The key insight you're landing on:**

Current transformers use enormous floating point vectors *because* they're trying to capture continuous gradations of meaning through statistical correlation. Your system says meaning is *discrete and structural* - it's which logical relationships are active, not where you fall on a continuous spectrum.

That's a genuinely different philosophical bet about the nature of meaning itself.

**The open question:**

How does the system *learn* which 56-bit pattern maps to "fire on a mountain" versus "fire the employee"? Transformers learn this through gradient descent on billions of examples.

What's your intuition for how a binary gate system would learn its encodings?

That's the piece that would make this a complete architecture.
